Amazon stopped accepting credit card payments for PPC in April 2026. For sellers who built their ad strategy around the 30-day payment float and 2–4% card rewards, the change felt less like a policy update and more like a margin grab.

Amazon softened the blow with a $2,500 one-time ad credit and, after significant seller backlash, pushed the rollout to August 1, 2026. But the core issue remains: most sellers switched to ACH billing and walked away from the rewards game entirely.

They didn't have to. There's a workaround gaining traction in eCom seller communities that keeps you on credit card, still earning 2X points on Amazon ad spend, fully tax-deductible, with clear net profit after fees. It's not a loophole. It's a straightforward reroute using a tool most sellers already know.

Here's how it works.

Amazon PPC Credit Card Removal: What Changed in April 2026

In early April 2026, Amazon began sending notices to a targeted segment of sellers announcing that advertising costs would be automatically deducted from sales proceeds. Credit cards would become backup payment methods only, used if proceeds couldn't cover ad spend.

The pushback was immediate. A group within Million Dollar Sellers organized a one-day ad boycott, and a poll of over 100 founders found that a majority expected the combined impact of the advertising change and delayed payouts to reduce their available cash by more than $100,000 (Modern Retail, April 2026).

Amazon responded on April 15 by deferring the rollout to August 1, 2026, citing the need to give affected advertisers "more time to prepare" (CNBC, April 2026). The underlying mechanics of the change have not changed.

The effect for affected accounts:

  • Ad costs deducted from proceeds before payout reaches your bank
  • No more 30-day credit card float
  • No more 2–4% rewards on Amazon ad spend
  • $2,500 one-time ad credit as a transition softener

Amazon's stated reasoning cited "cash flow management" for sellers. The more honest explanation is margin. Advertising is Amazon's fastest-growing revenue segment, and eliminating interchange fees on seller ad spend (which run between 1.5% and 2.5% on billions in ad volume) contributes directly to their bottom line.

"It's a double whammy. You lose the 30-day credit card float at the same time Amazon is already holding your sales proceeds for their own 30-day disbursement cycle. That's 60 days of working capital that quietly disappears."— Steven Pope, My Amazon Guy

For sellers who had built that combined buffer into their cash flow planning, it's a significant hit.

How Amazon's Ad Payment Change Affects Seller Cash Flow and Rewards

For small advertisers, the $2,500 credit actually helps. For larger sellers, the math turns ugly fast.

Take a seller spending $500K per year on Amazon PPC. At an average 2% card reward value, that's $10,000 in annual travel rewards, gone. At 4% (common with Amex Business cards on large charges), it's $20,000. My Amazon Guy estimates the rewards loss alone can exceed $15,000 a year for high-volume sellers, before you even account for the lost cash flow window.

Stack that against the other recent hits to seller margin:

  • Selling Partner API fees ($1,400 annual subscription, effective January 2026)
  • Mandatory prepaid return labels (January 2026)
  • Per-unit billing for FBA removals (February 2026)
  • Ongoing FBA fee hikes

Any single line item looks manageable. Combined, they're a real squeeze on already-thin eCom margins. Which is why finding a way to preserve the card rewards is worth the 30 minutes it takes to set up.

Worth noting: this pattern isn't isolated to Amazon. Google Ads notified a segment of advertisers in June 2024 that credit and debit cards would no longer be accepted as primary payment methods. Meta did the same in February 2026. Amazon's change is structurally similar to both (PPC Land, April 2026). The big ad platforms are all moving the same direction, which means finding stable workarounds matters more than ever.

The Bill.com Workaround: How to Keep Earning Credit Card Rewards on Amazon PPC

The workaround relies on two pieces:

  • An invoicing platform that accepts credit card payments and pays vendors on your behalf (Bill.com is the most widely used).
  • A catch-all rewards card with a strong multiplier on general business spend.

The flow is straightforward. Amazon invoices you for ad spend (pay-by-invoice accounts). You route that invoice through the invoicing platform. You pay the platform with your credit card. The platform pays Amazon.

1Amazon sends invoice
2Route to Bill.com
3Pay Bill.com with credit card
4Bill.com pays Amazon

You keep earning points. Amazon still gets paid. The platform takes a 2.9% transaction fee, which is fully tax-deductible as a business expense.

This approach has been used in other industries for years. It works on Amazon ads as long as you're on pay-by-invoice billing, which Amazon has confirmed remains available (PPC Land, April 2026). Credit to Jon Spektor for breaking down this approach and the card math in public eCom community discussions.

How Pay-by-Invoice Billing Works on Amazon Ads

Pay-by-invoice is an Amazon Ads billing option where Amazon sends monthly invoices for ad spend rather than charging a card or deducting from proceeds in real time. Not every seller account qualifies — Amazon has tightened eligibility in recent years — but for accounts that do, it's the gateway that makes this workaround possible. Confirm your eligibility inside Amazon Ads account settings before building the workflow below.

The Effective Fee After Taxes

Bill.com charges 2.9% on card payments. That sounds steep, but since it's a business expense, you deduct it. In the 37% federal tax bracket, the effective cost drops to roughly 1.8%. In states with income tax, it drops further.

2.9%→1.8%
Bill.com card fee drops to an effective 1.8% after the business-expense deduction in the 37% federal bracket — lower in states with income tax.

For the workaround to be profitable, your card rewards value needs to exceed 1.8%. Most quality business credit cards clear that bar easily.

Best Credit Cards for Amazon PPC Payments Through Bill.com

Here's the catch: Bill.com payments don't code as "advertising" to your card issuer. So cards with high multipliers tied to specific categories (like Amex Gold 4X on ads, or Chase Sapphire's bonus categories) won't give you those multipliers on Bill.com charges.

You need a catch-all card with strong flat rewards or a high multiplier on general large-ticket business spend. Three cards stand out.

Bill.com Amazon Ads Workaround: Eligibility, Risks, and Tax Considerations

A few honest caveats before you implement:

  • Eligibility varies: Not every seller account qualifies for pay-by-invoice billing. Amazon has tightened eligibility in recent years, particularly for smaller or newer sellers.
  • Tax bracket matters: The 37% bracket example is aggressive. At lower brackets, the effective fee is higher and the margin tightens.
  • State tax matters: Sellers in no-income-tax states (Florida, Texas, Washington) get a smaller deduction benefit. Sellers in California or New York get a larger one.
  • Keep clean records: This is a legitimate use of invoicing platforms, not a hack. But keep clean records and confirm with your CPA before implementing at scale.
  • Watch fee changes: Payment processors adjust fees. If Bill.com raises the card processing fee above ~3.2%, the math gets tight.
